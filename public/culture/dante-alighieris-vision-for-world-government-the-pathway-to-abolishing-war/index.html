700 Years Of Dante

Dante Alighieri's Vision for World Government: The Pathway to Abolishing War

Dante's death 700 years ago marked the end of an era. But his lasting legacy may be his vision for a world government, outlined in his work De Monarchia.

Dante’s death 700 years ago marked the end of an era. But his lasting legacy may be his vision for a world government, outlined in his work De Monarchia. Written in 1313, this work may be the first literature to present a comprehensive solution to the problem of war.

It‚Äôs difficult to imagine any sort of connection between the Russian invasion of Ukraine on February 24, 2022, and something that occurred in an unremarkable bedroom in Italy almost exactly 700 years prior. It has nothing whatsoever to do with the deep historical connections between Russia and Ukraine, at many times one and the same state, during the preceding seven centuries. But last autumn marked the 700th anniversary of the death of the great Italian poet Dante Alighieri, from malaria, in Ravenna, on September 14, 1321. His bones lie there still.

And though it‚Äôs almost wholly unknown today, Dante was arguably the first great political philosopher to make a systematic case that all wars between nations might someday be eliminated entirely, and that it is within the power of human ingenuity to cast war forever onto the rubble heap of history. Dante showed us the pathway out of the Ukraine war. Dante anticipated both federalism and democracy.

And Dante showed us how someday humanity might abolish war.

The great poet, of course, is considered one of the brightest stars in the firmament of humanity because of his immortal poem, ‚ÄúThe Divine Comedy.‚Äù Yet another wonderful new translation just arrived this year from the poet Mary Jo Bang. It contains three parts: ‚ÄúInferno,‚Äù ‚ÄúPurgatorio,‚Äù and ‚ÄúParadiso.‚Äù It‚Äôs universally held up as one of the most important works of literature in history. It‚Äôs deemed one of the primary progenitors of the Renaissance. It puts on display the full spectrum of human behavior, from dark depravity to divine benevolence‚Äîand suggests that any one of us can journey from the one to the other. Its poetic rhythms and deployment of vivid imagery are spellbinding. And because he wrote in the Italian dialect of the Florence of his day, Dante is considered today no less than the ‚ÄúFather of the Italian Language.‚Äù

And though his distant heirs today likely receive zero royalties, ‚ÄúDante‚Äôs Inferno‚Äù has even been made into a wildly-popular video game as well.

But if one considers not just literary excellence but also historical impact, it may be, in the very long run, that another more obscure work by Dante will provide humankind with an even greater tangible consequence. Because in a work called ‚ÄúDe Monarchia,‚Äù (‚ÄúOn Monarchy‚Äù)‚Äîthis one written in Latin in 1313‚ÄîDante put forth one singular grand idea about how the human race as a whole might one day organize its affairs. And it appears to be the very first work of literature to present in a comprehensive, coherent, and compelling fashion a solution to the problem of war. He called it ‚Äúworld government.‚Äù

It is difficult to understand why Dante‚Äôs manifesto about the political unification of humanity is so wholly unknown today. (Pope John XXII‚Äôs decree in 1329 that it be cast into the flames in the public square of Bologna might have something to do with that.)

Strobe Talbott expressed his exasperation about this obscurity in his 2008 book ‚ÄúThe Great Experiment: The Story of Ancient Empires, Modern States, and the Quest for a Global Nation.‚Äù Talbott was Bill Clinton‚Äôs Rhodes Scholar housemate at Oxford, a longtime TIME magazine correspondent, the number two State Department official for most of the Clinton administration, and president of the Brookings Institution think tank from 2002-2017. His book is an eloquent narrative history of the world state as a philosophical idea. And Talbott recalls and laments a ‚Äúhigh-brow international conference in Venice,‚Äù where his erudite Italian hosts could recite long passages of ‚ÄúThe Divine Comedy‚Äù from memory, but had not even heard of ‚ÄúDe Monarchia‚Äù!

Dante concerns himself in Books Two and Three of ‚ÄúDe Monarchia‚Äù with an issue that preoccupied many in 14th-century Europe‚Äîthe political power struggle between various secular rulers and the pope of the Roman Catholic Church. Dante in these two books (and in ‚ÄúThe Divine Comedy‚Äù as well) clearly came down on the side of the former, arguing that while the pope was responsible for the fate of one‚Äôs eternal soul, other individuals and institutions should govern our earthly pursuits. This writing is seen by many scholars as one of the important early lodestars that led finally to the separation of church and state in much of the world five centuries or so down the road.

In Book One, however, Dante concerns himself instead with the political power struggle between those autonomous secular rulers‚Äîand the perpetual state of war, or preparation for war, that prevailed among them all.

Speaking to his own age and speaking to us

Did Dante himself believe that this small work might prove someday even more consequential than ‚ÄúThe Divine Comedy‚Äù? The opening page of ‚ÄúDe Monarchia‚Äù suggests as much.

‚ÄúIf I have seen further,‚Äù said Isaac Newton in a 1675 letter to the scientist Robert Hooke, ‚Äúit is by standing on the shoulders of giants.‚Äù While the very first line of Dante‚Äôs ‚ÄúDe Monarchia‚Äù is not nearly as well-known as Newton‚Äôs famous tribute to the scientific geniuses who came before him, it expresses much the same sentiment: ‚ÄúAll men whose higher nature has endowed them with a love of truth obviously have the greatest interest in working for posterity, so that in return for the patrimony provided for them by their predecessors‚Äô labors they may make provision for the patrimony of future generations.‚Äù

(While Dante refers to ‚Äúmen‚Äù and ‚Äúmankind‚Äù throughout this work, today in a similar context we use terms like ‚Äúpeople‚Äù and ‚Äúhumankind.‚Äù Dante quite obviously means the latter, but nevertheless I will quote the text accurately whenever the former appears.)

Dante‚Äôs sentence, indeed, is richer and more elaborate than Newton‚Äôs. (Both have been called perhaps the smartest humans ever to walk the Earth‚Äîthough we might qualify that to recognize the 99% of all who ever lived, past and present, never given the opportunity to develop any of their intellectual gifts at all.) Not only do we ‚Äústand on the shoulders‚Äù of those who have gone before us, but also we hold living obligations to these departed souls. (And as we will see, Dante name checks several of his intellectual predecessors in ‚ÄúDe Monarchia.‚Äù) To honor what they have provided to us, we must provide them with something ‚Äúin return.‚Äù How might we do that? By taking the torch they have passed forward to us, says Dante, by holding it aloft and endeavoring to make it burn even more robustly, and by handing it off then to those who will come after us.

It is hard to imagine a more powerful definition of a meaningful life than that.

Dante then lays out how he proposes, in ‚ÄúDe Monarchia,‚Äù to contribute to ‚Äúthe public weal.‚Äù Not for him a mere elaboration upon ‚Äúa theorem in Euclid,‚Äù or yet another exploration of ‚Äútrue happiness which Aristotle has already shown,‚Äù or extolling ‚Äúold age as Cicero did.‚Äù No, says Dante, ‚ÄúI wish not to be charged with burying my talent ‚Ä¶ [So] I endeavor ‚Ä¶ to bear fruit by publishing truths that have not been attempted by others.‚Äù And which uncultivated verity does he choose?

‚ÄúThe knowledge of a single temporal government over mankind is most important and least explored ‚Ä¶ (and) has been neglected by all.‚Äù

And then, Dante says something remarkable for its open display of abundant ambition.

‚ÄúI therefore propose to drag it from its hiding place, in order that my alertness ‚Ä¶ may bring me the glory of being the first to win this great prize.‚Äù

Hardly anyone would openly proclaim such a thing today. Public modesty is the fashion of our age. One invariably balks when asked to assess one‚Äôs own contributions in most any endeavor. Are you the greatest shooter of a basketball of all time Stephen Curry? ‚ÄúThat‚Äôs for others to judge.‚Äù Many writers, thinkers, and actors in many arenas may privately hope or believe that their contributions to the larger world, whatever they may be, may ‚Äúbring them the glory.‚Äù But no one openly says so.

Why did Dante? Perhaps an extravagant confidence or even cockiness was not frowned upon then and there, in the same way as here and now. Perhaps he refused to engage in what today we call ‚Äúfalse modesty.‚Äù Or perhaps Dante simply possessed such conceit in a greater quantity than almost anyone alive today. As the ancient schoolyard taunt goes: ‚ÄúIt ain‚Äôt bragging if it‚Äôs true.‚Äù Perhaps Dante recognized that his literary and creative achievements would remain as luminescent as anyone who has ever lived.

Or perhaps Dante simply possessed such conceit in a greater quantity than almost anyone alive today.

And yet, seven centuries on, it is undeniable that ‚ÄúDe Monarchia‚Äù failed to bring Dante any kind of glory whatsoever. His openly stated aspiration remains wholly unfulfilled. Many educated and literate individuals in the 21st century have heard of ‚ÄúThe Divine Comedy.‚Äù Some of those have read it from beginning to end. A few of them have even fully understood it. (I do not count myself among the latter.) But today, 700 years later, virtually no one thinks of Dante as ‚Äúthe first to win the great prize‚Äù of revealing to the world the Big Idea that world government might banish war from the human condition forever. As Strobe Talbott discovered, hardly anyone, even among the well-read and privileged, has even ever heard of ‚ÄúDe Monarchia.‚Äù And our human race doesn‚Äôt seem a whole lot closer to bringing about ‚Äúa single temporal government over mankind‚Äù today than we were when Dante died 700 years ago.

But what if we fast forward another seven centuries, let‚Äôs say to the year 2722? It doesn‚Äôt strike one as wholly fanciful to suppose that humanity by then will have put war behind itself, gotten a grip on our greatest transnational challenges (climate change paramount but hardly alone among them), and somehow inaugurated the spiritual, political and constitutional unification of humankind. One might even posit that if we don‚Äôt manage somehow to move in that direction long before then, and learn how to manage as a whole the complex affairs of our one human race, seven centuries from now we may not find any descendants of ours around at all.

But if we do? Then perhaps it is not too farfetched to suppose that those denizens of a future age will see ‚ÄúDe Monarchia‚Äù as not just a towering literary achievement like ‚ÄúThe Divine Comedy,‚Äù but a work of literature that, in the very long run, helped to move human history in the direction of its destiny.

Why a world government?

The central idea of Dante‚Äôs ‚ÄúDe Monarchia,‚Äù like so much of the literature on the basic causes of peace and war that followed in subsequent centuries, is the distinction between law and anarchy. Dante observes that while there are laws that govern relations within individual political communities, no such laws exist to govern relations among political communities. The result of the absence of such enforceable law is a continual state of war between those separate political communities. Such a state was defined by Dante in much the same way as it would be a couple of centuries later by Thomas Hobbes and many others: not just violent conflict, but the absolute necessity in the absence of a superior legal order to constantly prepare for the next violent conflict to come.

And yet, Dante asks, why can‚Äôt there be laws on that largest level of human community? If we could find a way to establish the world rule of law‚Äîthrough the establishment of world government‚Äîwe would bring about the end of that state of perpetual war.

Dante signals the logical case he intends to construct with the titles of his opening chapters. ‚ÄúThe goal of human civilization [Chapter 2] ‚Ä¶ is the realization of man‚Äôs ability to grow in intelligence [Chapter 3]. The best means toward this end is universal peace [Chapter 4]. To achieve this state of universal well-being a single world government is necessary [Chapter 5].‚Äù ‚ÄúDe Monarchia‚Äù consists of 16 such focused individual chapters. He concludes one with this statement: ‚ÄúHence it follows that mankind lives best under a single government, and therefore that such a government is necessary for the well-being of the world.‚Äù As if he is teaching a class on symbolic logic to a room full of college undergraduates, Dante ends almost every one of the 16 chapters with a similar forthright assertion. No hidden meanings in parables that one must struggle to puzzle out. No camels passing through the eye of a needle. Dante is convinced that a single world state could bring about permanent world peace. And in ‚ÄúDe Monarchia‚Äù he sets out not just to convince us of that but to ‚Äúprove‚Äù his assertion‚Äîa word he uses repeatedly throughout his manuscript.

Dante launches directly into illuminating the logic of anarchy in the absence of higher authority. Conflict, Dante tells us, is inevitable in human relations. This is true of interactions between both individuals and groups. Without some way to resolve such conflicts in a peaceful manner, they likely will be‚Äîas they have been throughout all history‚Äîsettled in a less-peaceful manner.

Conflict, Dante tells us, is inevitable in human relations. This is true of interactions between both individuals and groups.

Between any two governments, neither of which is in any way subordinate to the other, contention can arise ‚Ä¶ Therefore there should be adjudication between them ‚Ä¶ a third and wider power which can rule both within its own jurisdiction. ‚Ä¶ We must arrive at a first and supreme judge for whom all contentions are judiciable ‚Ä¶ and this will be our world governor or emperor.

Now as terrifying as the phrase ‚Äúworld emperor‚Äù might seem, as quick as it might be to summon images of a universal Stalin or Hitler or Genghis, consider closely the words that Dante deploys in this passage: ‚Äújudge,‚Äù ‚Äújurisdiction,‚Äù ‚Äújudiciable contentions.‚Äù The possibility of a world government becoming a global dictatorship is not one to be just casually dismissed. But it‚Äôs a danger that Dante clearly wanted to forestall. How can we know that?

Because astonishingly, in ‚ÄúDe Monarchia,‚Äù nearly 500 years before the American and French Revolutions, Dante presaged both the republican principles and the federative structures that most of the nations of the world have adopted in our own age.

No world tyranny, but a carefully-balanced world authority

‚ÄúIt should be clearly understood that not every little regulation for every city could come directly from the world government ‚Ä¶ nations, states, and cities have their own internal concerns which require special laws.‚Äù

Obviously, it is those on the ground, in those localities, who are best acquainted with those local concerns‚Äîand best equipped to devise the consequent necessary ‚Äúlittle regulations.‚Äù Seven centuries later, we call that ‚Äúsubsidiarity‚Äù or ‚Äúfederalism.‚Äù

And who, in those nations, states and cities, should be devising and applying those special laws? Local leaders, says Dante, ‚Äúraised to office by the consent of others.‚Äù Seven centuries later, we call that ‚Äúdemocracy.‚Äù

And when Dante offers up a couple of examples to illustrate his thesis, he reveals a bit of what the people of his own time and place knew about world geography a couple of centuries before Columbus. The ‚ÄúScythians‚Äù he writes, must govern themselves in certain ways because of an ‚Äúalmost intolerable, freezing cold.‚Äù ‚ÄúGaramantes,‚Äù on the other hand, choose very different practices because they live ‚Äúbelow the equinoctial circle, where daylight and dark of night are always balanced, and where the excessive heat makes clothes unendurable.‚Äù

Take note, 21st-century conservative Christians who, in the view of some, concern themselves excessively with issues of ‚Äúpublic morality.‚Äù Apparently, according to arguably the greatest of all medieval Christian thinkers, no world government should ever ban public nudity!

Those ‚ÄúScythians‚Äù lived in what we would describe as the former Soviet Union, in the seemingly endless forests and steppes to the north and east of the Black Sea, and are referred to often in Greco-Roman times. The reference to the ‚ÄúGaramantes‚Äù is a bit trickier. They were a civilization that first appeared in modern-day Libya about 1000 BCE. Yet they had long since disappeared by Dante‚Äôs day. Moreover, ‚Äúequinoctial circle‚Äù is another term for the equator, and Dante indicates as much by his clear understanding of the ‚Äúbalance‚Äù between day and night. The equator, of course, runs not through the Libya of today, but the heart of Africa a good 1000 miles to the south. So Dante appears to be referring to sub-Saharan Africans, which Italians in the early-14th century knew very little about. But enough, presumably, to insist that if they were someday to be incorporated into a world state, they should be allowed to devise their own ‚Äúspecial laws‚Äù for their own ‚Äúinternal concerns!‚Äù

And yet, these local customs and governing mechanisms, Dante makes clear, would have to be consistent with the larger laws of the world state. And as example for this point the great poet summons Moses. The best guess of modern historians is that the Jewish patriarch lived roughly 1300 BCE. But 2600 years later, Dante informs us that Moses skillfully sustained the same kind of delicate poise that must prevail in a future world state: ‚ÄúMoses followed this pattern in the law which he composed, for having chosen the chiefs of the several tribes, he left them the lesser judgements, reserving to himself alone the higher and more general.‚Äù

This, we might posit, is why Moses instructed his followers to obey ten commandments, not ten thousand. The chiefs, however, in rendering those ‚Äúlesser‚Äù judgements, must act consistently with those ten. ‚ÄúThese common norms,‚Äù says Dante, ‚Äúwere then used by the tribal chiefs according to their special needs‚Äù (emphasis added).

The goal of human endeavor

Like so many later thinkers about the world government idea, Dante sees it hardly as an end in itself‚Äîglorious though perpetual peace might seem to our eternally warring world‚Äîbut a means to a grander end still. In this regard he is much like H.G. Wells, who six centuries later would forecast that what he called ‚Äúa federation of all humanity‚Äù would bring about ‚Äúa new phase in human history.‚Äù What is that grander end for Dante?

Like so many later thinkers about the world government idea, Dante sees it hardly as an end in itself‚Äîglorious though perpetual peace might seem to our eternally warring world‚Äîbut a means to a grander end still.

This goal is proved to be the realization of man‚Äôs ability to grow in intelligence.

Now many today would protest immediately that so much else needs to be done, for so many in our world of nearly eight billion souls, before we can start tutoring them on the most arcane points of philosophy or mathematics or high art. A habitable planet that hasn‚Äôt been driven completely haywire by climate change. Clean air. Clean water. Healthy food. Adequate, comfortable, perhaps even beautiful places to live. Safety, especially for the young, from sexual abuse. Safety for everyone from violence and fear of physical assault. An ability to make a living, to receive meaningful compensation for an honest day‚Äôs labor. An opportunity to live a life of dignity and purpose. Perhaps even a chance to pursue one‚Äôs dreams.

Yet, though Dante doesn‚Äôt put it in those terms, one feels certain that he would agree. All these things are necessary and desirable, and for Dante, the bedrock variable that allows any individual to pursue such things is the opportunity ‚Äúto grow in intelligence.‚Äù

Moreover, Dante draws a distinction between particular individuals expanding their knowledge, and the human race as a whole doing much the same thing. ‚ÄúThere is some proper function for the whole of mankind as an organized multitude which cannot be achieved by any single man, or family, or neighborhood, or city, or state ‚Ä¶ There must be a multitude ‚Ä¶ through whom this whole power can be actualized.‚Äù

Now it is certainly not the case today that there is absolutely no cross-border intellectual collaboration of any kind. But plenty of such potential collaborations are constrained by international borders and conflict. Moreover, so much intellectual effort gets diverted away from international collaboration and diverted instead into national competition‚Äîmost especially in the military and ‚Äúnational security‚Äù realms. When that kind of rivalry is eliminated forever, says Dante, it will be not just individuals, but the collective global brain, which can then move toward its highest potential in ways we probably cannot begin to imagine.

So how do individuals often achieve their own intellectual enhancement, and enlargement, and perhaps even original breakthroughs? And how might humanity do the same? Dante‚Äôs answer seems spectacularly obvious: ‚ÄúSince individual men find that they grow in prudence and wisdom when they can sit quietly, it is evident that mankind, too, is most free and easy to carry on its work when it enjoys the quiet and tranquility of peace.‚Äù

Peace and quiet. When we as individuals can find some, our brains begin to clear, our minds begin to work, and sometimes our creativity begins to soar. It sounds so simple, and yet, for so many, it is so often elusive. Dante, who was of course a devout Christian, summons the heritage of Christianity to make the point. Yet his analysis is every bit as relevant for non-Christians and non-believers in any kind of deity alike.

The good news from the shepherds, Dante says, was ‚Äúnot of riches, nor pleasures, nor honors, nor long life, nor health, nor strength, nor beauty.‚Äù It was instead, he insists, quoting from the Book of Luke, ‚Äúpeace to men of good will.‚Äù Indeed, says Dante, the standard greeting given by Jesus Christ was ‚Äúpeace be with you.‚Äù He calls it ‚Äúthe supreme salutation.‚Äù It is the greeting that the disciples of Jesus adopted as their own. And it is the same greeting that all Roman Catholics today, about 2/3 of the way through each Sunday mass, are asked to give to their fellow parishioners sitting around them in the adjacent pews.

We often attain our highest and best moments, and our greatest achievements, when we can sit down and sit still. Surely, says Dante, if all the human race, for the first time in history, could similarly ‚Äúsit quietly,‚Äù and not have to live perpetually in fear of conquest, and not have to concern itself with the requirements of military security, this year and next year and every year after that, the same will be true ‚Äúfor the whole of mankind as an organized multitude.‚Äù

Step by step, almost as if he is constructing a mathematical theorem, Dante has built the road to the redemption of humanity. World government will bring universal peace. Such peace will bring the quiet life. That quiet life, for human individuals and the human race alike, will bring a new and unprecedented ‚Äúgrowth in intelligence.‚Äù And that growth will generate a creative spark that will allow at last the full unfolding of the capacities of the human mind, and the full flowering of human civilization.

Dante, it seems, in drawing the analogy between quiet human beings and a quiet human race, crafted for us in ‚ÄúDe Monarchia‚Äù a parallel of perfection.

The shoulders of giants

Moses is not the only intellectual predecessor summoned by Dante in ‚ÄúDe Monarchia.‚Äù The late University of Chicago President Robert Maynard Hutchins (who also chaired the 1948 ‚ÄúCommittee to Frame a World Constitution‚Äù) spoke often of ‚Äúthe great conversation‚Äù‚Äîgreat minds engaging each other about great ideas across the gulf of many centuries. In this spirit Dante repeatedly cites Aristotle (in both ‚ÄúDe Monarchia‚Äù and ‚ÄúThe Divine Comedy‚Äù), 16 centuries prior, to drive home his points. ‚ÄúThings hate to be in disorder,‚Äù says Dante quoting Aristotle, ‚Äúbut a plurality of authorities is disorder; therefore, authority is single.‚Äù But when one goes in search of this passage in Aristotle, and finds it in his ‚ÄúMetaphysics,‚Äù we see that the philosopher there is himself quoting Homer from ‚ÄúThe Iliad‚Äù‚Äîwhich as best we know was written four or five centuries before Aristotle. (Or, more precisely, that‚Äôs when ‚ÄúThe Iliad‚Äù was written down, recording oral traditions that originated perhaps four or five centuries earlier than that!)

Now the phrase ‚Äúauthority is single‚Äù is most problematic when expressed in the present tense. Authority was certainly not ‚Äúsingle‚Äù in the lands around the Aegean Sea circa 1200 or 1300 BCE, otherwise there would have been no dreadful war between the Mycenaean Greeks and the Trojans over a beguiling woman named Helen. Authority was certainly not ‚Äúsingle‚Äù in the lands of the Peloponnesus during Aristotle‚Äôs lifetime (384 to 322 BCE), otherwise there would have been no catastrophic war between Athens and Sparta just a few decades before Aristotle was born. So what if we take the liberty of altering the text from the present to the future tense? What if we suppose that the sentence might have been intended‚Äîpossibly by Homer, perhaps by Aristotle, and almost certainly seen in its context by Dante‚Äîas prescription rather than description? ‚ÄúThings hate to be in disorder, but a plurality of authorities is disorder,‚Äù perhaps sang some ancient bard, perhaps as long as 33 centuries ago, perhaps as she or he observed the denizens of the Aegean world slaughtering each other incessantly during the grueling and protracted Trojan War. ‚ÄúTherefore authority,‚Äù she or he pleaded, ‚Äúmust someday become single.‚Äù

Was this single sentence merely a throwaway line in ‚ÄúThe Iliad‚Äù? Perhaps. And yet, one of our human heritage‚Äôs great geniuses, Aristotle, found it important enough to quote five centuries later. And another great genius, Dante, chose to highlight it again 16 centuries after that. So perhaps it is not too much of a stretch for us to suppose that the first glimmer of the idea that a ‚Äúsingle authority‚Äù can bring an end to war appeared in the first known work of Western literature, Homer‚Äôs ‚ÄúIliad‚Äù‚Äîwhich, as every undergraduate learns in World Civilization 101, is a piece of literature quintessentially about the human condition during the (so far) eternal times of war.

But Can it Ever Happen? Dante Says Yes, Because Once it Did Happen

Dante brings Book One of ‚ÄúDe Monarchia‚Äù to a close with the same kind of poetic brilliance and cri de coeur he displayed in ‚ÄúThe Divine Comedy,‚Äù which has brought him, if not the glory of delivering eternal world peace to all subsequent generations, nonetheless still an eternal fame. And he does so by insisting that his vision is the opposite of some forever unattainable utopia‚Äîbecause in fact it had already once come to pass. When? During the days of Pax Romana.

Dante was likely not blind to the flaws and depredations of Ancient Rome. He certainly knew of the cruelties of slavery (which of course were not unique in the ancient world to Rome). He certainly knew that in that age (as well as in Dante‚Äôs), women almost never obtained the same kind of opportunities to develop their minds and lead fulfilling meaningful lives as (some) men. He certainly knew that the magnificent edifice of the Roman Empire had been constructed by the hard hammer of Roman conquest, unfolding over the course of several centuries.

And yet the peace that resulted within that vast empire, brought about by a single unified political authority, seems to Dante to be no less than the greatest achievement in all of human history.

During the Augustan Empire there prevailed a maximum of world peace ‚Ä¶ [but] miseries have overtaken man since we departed from that golden age. ‚Ä¶ For if we recall all the ages and conditions of men ‚Ä¶ we shall find that not until the time of Augustus was there a complete and single world government which pacified the world. Now we must acknowledge that Dante uses terms here that he undoubtedly knew to be hyperbolic. ‚ÄúPacified the world.‚Äù ‚ÄúMankind.‚Äù ‚ÄúA complete and single world government.‚Äù Obviously Roman rule and its consequent Pax Romana did not extend over the entire planet. So too did the Romans themselves.

And yet, to the Romans themselves, at their empire‚Äôs height of extent and power and durability, it must have seemed like it was in effect all of humankind that mattered to them, all of the world that needed to be governed by them. They conquered and reigned over everything west of Rome to the shores of the vast Atlantic Ocean, and all the way as far north as Scotland. They completely encircled the Mediterranean Sea. They extended their domain as far east as the Caspian Sea, the Persian Gulf, and the Arabian Sea. They controlled completely the three great rivers that had served as the earliest cradles of civilization in antiquity: the Nile, the Tigris, and the Euphrates. They interacted and somehow coexisted peacefully with only one other entity that we might today consider a state‚Äîthe Parthian Empire (roughly modern-day Iran), to the east of the vast lands of the Levant, which Rome had brought under its sway (roughly modern-day Israel, Lebanon, Syria, Jordan, and Iraq).
